Silence in Culture

Context

Languages in spoken and written form assume a certain level of knowledge.

High Context assumes that the recipient already possesses a working body of knowledge. Therefore, more things can be implied.

Low Context assumes that the reciepent knows little. Therefore, more information needs to be made explicit.

Use in Language

Silence is an especially powerful tool in Eastern spoken language.
Below are two Japenese proverbs on the value of silence:

  • kiji mo nakazuba utaremi:
    "silence keeps one safe" (Jones 18)

  • mono ieba kuchibiru samushi aki no kaze:
    “it is better to leave many things unsaid” (Jones 18)

Silence also provides an opportunity to "save face," and not do any damage to reputation.
For example, in a classroom environment, rather than saying "I don't know," or making up an answer on the spot, it is not considered impolite to decline answering a question.

What Makes Awkward Silence... Awkward?

"High-context cultures tend to encode messages in the context of the verbal utterance
(including the non-verbal communication) rather than the verbal utterances themselves.”
