Silence as a Tool of Control

In Personal Relationships

The Silent Treatment

The Silent Treatment is a form of abuse by way of not speaking or offering up any kind of communication. It is typical with narcissists, as it is a way of exacting control, avoiding confrontation, and therefore making the recipient of the abuse feel that their own position is wrong.

In this manner silence is meant to frustrate and disorient the target.
Ultimately, the Silent Treatment is a tactic of avoiding an actual solution, as grievances are not being voiced.

The person enacting the Silent Treatment often feels a higher sense of power over the situation.

Due to the ambiguity of silence, the target often experiences anxiousness and a lowered sense of self-esteem due to this unproductive method of conflict resolution. Long-term negative effects are common, especially in children who experienced the silent treatment as punishment.

(Rittenour 2-3)

In Society

The Sunken Place— an institutional form of silencing

The Silent Treatement is not just limited to interpersonal relationships.

In the U.S., the voices of marginalized people (women, POC, LGBTQ+, etc.,) have long been kept silent by those in power— typically, white, well-off, land-owning men. And unfortunately, this still is largely the case.

Women through history have been discouraged from speaking, being assertive, and confident— as these characteristics are considered to be unfeminine and challenge the status quo (Rodriguez 113).

In other words, it makes the rich white guys uncomfortable.


This institutional Silent Treatment is enacted by withholding voting rights, bodily autonomy, ability to own land and hold office, etc.. As a result, and by design, needs have gone unmet, and individual liberties have been hindered.

For over 400 years in the United States, there was a system of absolute control over every aspect of a person's life with no chance for autonomy.

The film Get Out illustrates the active silencing perpetuated upon Black Americans by those in positions of power. The "place" where one is made voiceless and unable to move is called "The Sunken Place."



The imagery in this scene takes many artistic liberties to illustrate lack of autonomy.
Jordan Peele's Get Out takes place in modern day, but it seeks to illustrate how (seemingly) antiquated systems and beliefs have persisted through time, and how silencing of a voice can strip people from their own autonomy.

Silence as Complicity

In the face of adversity, those that refuse to speak out are complicit.

A demonstrated unwillingness to speak; or a commitment to maintaining willful ignorance can be a way of exacting a positionality of "I don't have to discuss this as it does not exist in my experience, therefore it does not exist at all." This scenario is common today.

Silence as Survival

Silence can also be used as a survival tool, as it masks emotion. Examples in history may be by Japanese-American women and their not speaking on experiences in internment camps; enslaved African-Americans are other examples. In these cases silence was used as a way of concealing outrage, as punitive systems do not tolerate outrage.

...an “inherited legacy of submission and simply not showing one’s feelings (Rodriguez 113).”
